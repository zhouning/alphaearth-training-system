\begin{abstract}
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA, adapters, and BitFit have become standard tools for adapting large pre-trained models in natural language processing and computer vision. However, it remains unclear whether the same PEFT rankings transfer to geospatial foundation models, which differ in both architecture and input structure. In this work, we present the first systematic benchmark of PEFT methods on Prithvi-100M under heterogeneous input settings. We evaluate seven adaptation strategies across five modality configurations on EuroSAT, validate the main findings on BigEarthNet-S2, and further extend the benchmark to semantic segmentation on LandCover.ai. In total, the study covers 109 experiments, including a full fine-tuning baseline, a diagnostic LoRA split-QKV ablation, and a dense-prediction replication.

Our results reveal three consistent findings. First, LoRA provides virtually no gain over linear probing across both EuroSAT and BigEarthNet-S2. A diagnostic ablation shows that this failure is caused by two compounding factors: a fused-QKV implementation trap in standard PyTorch attention layers, and the fact that low-rank adaptation remains suboptimal even after Q/K/V are correctly exposed. Second, Houlsby adapters consistently dominate all other PEFT methods, reaching 94.5\% of full fine-tuning performance on EuroSAT while using only 1.4\% of the trainable parameters. Third, modality selection matters more than PEFT choice: multi-spectral inputs consistently outperform RGB subsets across methods and datasets. The LandCover.ai extension confirms that the Houlsby $\gg$ LoRA $\approx$ Linear~Probe ranking is not specific to classification: on dense prediction, the Houlsby advantage widens to $+155\%$ relative mIoU, while LoRA still collapses to the linear-probe baseline to the fourth decimal place.

These results suggest that PEFT intuitions from mainstream NLP and vision cannot be transferred directly to geospatial foundation models without validation. Our benchmark provides both empirical guidance for practitioners and a reproducible experimental framework for future GeoFM adaptation research.
\end{abstract}
